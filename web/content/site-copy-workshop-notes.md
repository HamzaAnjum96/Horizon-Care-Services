## 4) Competitor landscape: how others position, and where we can win

**Chair prompt:** “Let’s talk competitors honestly. No names needed. What patterns do you see?”

### Observed competitor patterns (panel synthesis)
1. **Credential-heavy but process-light pages**
   - Lots of logos and generic trust statements.
   - Limited explanation of how requirements are handled in practice.

2. **Speed-only claims**
   - Strong emphasis on ‘urgent cover in minutes’.
   - Often unclear on fit quality and post-placement support.

3. **Candidate-first messaging overshadowing client needs**
   - Recruitment appeal dominates homepage.
   - Client conversion path is less explicit.

4. **Overstated certainty**
   - Language implies universal availability.
   - Little acknowledgement of constraints or safe limits.

### Where Horizon can differentiate

**Operations Director (Daniel):** “By being explicit about decisions and constraints. That’s rare and useful.”

**Senior Coordinator (Farah):** “Also by making the communication model visible: who updates, when updates happen, and what happens if requirements change.”

**NHS perspective (Michael):** “If you show process maturity without inflated claims, you stand out immediately.”

**Chair conclusion:** Differentiate on operational honesty + structured workflow + accountability after placement, not vague superiority claims.

## 5) What we should say (and avoid saying)

### 5.1 Approved message pillars

1. **Requirement-first briefing**
   - Start with context, risk, role scope, escalation and handover expectations.

2. **Setting-fit matching**
   - Explicitly avoid treating workers as interchangeable.

3. **Clear confirmation discipline**
   - Availability, constraints, and next steps communicated clearly.

4. **After-placement follow-through**
   - Ongoing support when shifts change or extend.

5. **Honest capacity boundaries**
   - Straight answers when requirements cannot be met safely.

### 5.2 Claims to avoid without proof

- Quantified outcomes (fill rates, response times, retention rates) unless evidence is available and maintained.
- Regulatory/certification claims not currently documented for publication.
- Superlatives (“best”, “leading”, “number one”) without robust substantiation.

**Chair directive:** “Every claim in public copy must map to a behavior the operations team can deliver consistently.”

## 6) Information architecture and page roles

**PM (Marcus) introduces model:** “Each page must have one job, one primary audience, one main CTA.”

### Homepage role
- **Primary audience:** Organisations needing staffing support.
- **Primary CTA:** Request staffing support / discuss requirement.
- **Must include:** clear who/where served, operating method snapshot, confidence in next-step process.

### Services page role
- **Primary audience:** Buyers validating service relevance.
- **Primary CTA:** Submit requirement with setting details.
- **Must include:** staff categories, supported settings, matching approach, practical request workflow.

### About page role
- **Primary audience:** Trust validators and referred decision makers.
- **Primary CTA:** Contact team for requirement discussion.
- **Must include:** operating philosophy, accountability behaviors, continuity mindset.

### Work For Us page role
- **Primary audience:** candidates/professionals.
- **Primary CTA:** Apply or send CV with role preferences.
- **Must include:** what good shift experience looks like, expectations, support model, communication standards.

**Chair conclusion:** Keep pages distinct; remove duplication where it adds no decision value.

## 7) Conversion design and CTA language

**Chair question:** “What CTA wording sounds realistic and useful?”

**Care Home Manager (Sarah):** “Request staff is okay, but ‘Discuss your requirement’ lowers friction when needs are complex.”

**LA Lead (Priya S.):** “Agreed. We often need to sense-check before formal request.”

**SEO Specialist (Owen):** “Dual CTA can work if hierarchy is clear: primary action + consultative secondary action.”

### CTA principles agreed
- Primary CTA verbs: **Request**, **Discuss**, **Apply**, **Send**.
- Avoid vague CTA labels like “Learn more” where conversion intent is strong.
- Clarify what happens after click (response expectations and required input fields).

### Suggested microcopy for forms
- “Tell us the setting, role, urgency, and any non-negotiables.”
- “We’ll confirm availability, constraints, and next steps.”

**Chair conclusion:** CTAs should reduce uncertainty about the first interaction, not just collect clicks.

## 8) Trust signals without inflated claims

**Chair:** “We need trust signals that are true, maintainable, and non-theatrical.”

### Strong trust signal candidates
- Company registration details.
- Clear coverage areas.
- Explicit process steps.
- Transparent role categories and settings supported.
- Contact channels that imply responsiveness.

### Conditional trust signals (only if evidence maintained)
- Specific response windows.
- Placement continuity statistics.
- Candidate satisfaction statements.

**Finance Manager (Ruth):** “If we publish time promises, we need operational reporting to defend them.”

**Quality Lead (Nisha):** “Let’s avoid hard numbers for now unless we create a governance routine.”

**Chair conclusion:** Use process and transparency as core trust architecture; add metrics only when governance is ready.

## 9) Objection handling matrix

### Buyer objections

**Objection 1: “Will this create more admin for my team?”**  
**Answer direction:** Show briefing efficiency, single-point updates, and clear confirmations.

**Objection 2: “Are you just sending whoever is available?”**  
**Answer direction:** Explain setting-fit matching criteria and clarification steps.

**Objection 3: “What happens when plans change?”**  
**Answer direction:** Describe escalation and re-plan support after placement.

**Objection 4: “Can I trust your promises?”**  
**Answer direction:** Use measured, specific language and avoid over-claiming.

### Candidate objections

**Objection 1: “Will shifts be clearly briefed?”**  
**Answer direction:** Publish expectation that role details and reporting lines are clarified before attendance.

**Objection 2: “Will someone be reachable?”**  
**Answer direction:** Emphasize named contact and escalation support.

**Objection 3: “Is pay process transparent?”**  
**Answer direction:** Use plain language on rate confirmation and payment cycle consistency.

**Chair conclusion:** Copy should proactively answer objections in-page, not rely on contact forms to do the heavy lifting.

## 12) Proposed content modules to add or expand

1. **How we handle a requirement (4-step strip)**
   - Brief → Match → Confirm → Follow-through.

2. **What we need from you to move quickly**
   - Setting, role, urgency, non-negotiables, escalation contact.

3. **When requirements change**
   - Explain re-plan process and update cadence.

4. **Who this service is for / not for**
   - Honest fit guidance to reduce low-quality enquiries.

5. **Candidate shift-readiness expectations**
   - What professionals should provide and what they can expect in return.

6. **Plain-language ‘How we differ’ module**
   - No comparisons; only operating principles.

## 13) Risk review

**Chair asks:** “What could go wrong if we add more detail?”

- **Risk:** Overpromising implied by precise language.  
  **Mitigation:** Use capability language tied to operations, avoid hard performance numbers.

- **Risk:** Too much text reduces readability.  
  **Mitigation:** Break into modules, headers, bullets, and concise explanatory paragraphs.

- **Risk:** Candidate page drifts into client messaging.  
  **Mitigation:** Keep each page’s conversion goal explicit.

- **Risk:** Inconsistent tone across pages.  
  **Mitigation:** Implement editorial style checklist before publish.

## 14) Editorial checklist agreed for next drafting pass

Before publishing any section, confirm:
1. Is the audience explicit?
2. Is the conversion action explicit?
3. Does the section describe a real behavior/process?
4. Could this sentence appear on any agency site unchanged? If yes, rewrite.
5. Are we avoiding unverified claims and inflated superlatives?
6. Is there clear next-step language?
7. Is the section skimmable (header + short blocks + list where useful)?
